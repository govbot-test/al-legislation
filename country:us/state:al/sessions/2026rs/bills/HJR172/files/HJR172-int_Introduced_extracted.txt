4 HJR___ CELEBRATING THE LIFE AND LEGACY OF REVEREND JESSE
5 JACKSON.
6
7 WHEREAS, it is with profound sorrow that we record
8 the passing of Civil Rights leader Reverend Jesse Jackson on
9 February 17, 2026, at the age of 84; we celebrate his legacy
10 of servant leadership and advocacy with thanksgiving, even
11 as we mourn his devastating loss; and
12 WHEREAS, Jesse Burns was born on October 8, 1941, in
13 Greenville, South Carolina, to Helen Burns, who married
14 Charles Henry Jackson two years after Jesse's birth; he took
15 the surname of his stepfather, who adopted him when he was
16 15; together, his parents raised him in a loving and
17 supportive home; his grandmother, Tibby, encouraged in him a
18 sense of self-belief and would often borrow books for him to
19 read; and
20 WHEREAS, Reverend Jackson began his higher education
21 at the University of Illinois on an athletic scholarship;
22 his first protest occurred when he was visiting home during
23 his freshman year and could not borrow a book from the
24 white-only library; he organized a sit-in with seven other
25 students in protest, the first of many peaceful protests
26 that he organized and participated in throughout his life;
27 and

39 Operation PUSH, which he later expanded to include other
40 marginalized people and renamed the organization the Rainbow
41 PUSH Coalition; throughout the 1970s and 1980s, Reverend
42 Jackson grew to be a prominent figure in the Civil Rights
43 Movement, maintaining forward momentum in sync with the
44 Movement as it progressed with the victorious passage of
45 each civil rights act; and
46 WHEREAS, Reverend Jackson twice made a bid for the
47 presidency in 1984 and 1988; his first campaign had
48 significant support with more than one million new
49 registered voters and winning 3.5 million votes; he was a
50 gifted orator, delivering impassioned speeches, including an
51 emotional crescendo at the 1988 Democratic National
52 Convention, an appeal that placed him second behind Michael
53 Dukakis; until the election of Barack Obama in 2008, he was
54 the most successful Black candidate for the highest office;
55 and
